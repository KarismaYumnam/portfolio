Comparison of remote sensing platforms for spatiotemporal canopy vigor monitoring in apple orchards

This is a passion project conducted by Karisma Yumnam in 2023

Acknowledgements: Washington State University, Gajanan Kothawade, Lav R. Khot


With the adaptation of precision agriculture technologies in the recent years, there had been increasing need for timely crop information for effective management practices. The Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) platforms were used for rapid crop assessment as it offers high spatial resolution and convenient data acquisition. However, it has limited capabilities for large-scale monitoring. In the recent years, the spatial resolution of satellite imageries has drastically improved, thus providing the potential to provide reliable and scalable decision support for precision agriculture. However, the accuracy of the satellite imageries needs to be evaluated especially, for assessing in-field variability.

The current study systematically assessed the Pleiades, PlanetScope and Sentinel-2 satellite platforms for apple orchards canopy vigor monitoring at two commercial apple orchards. Additionally, the management unit zones (MUZs) were developed for foliar chemical applications considering data from different platforms. Results showed that the satellite platforms underestimated the NDVI values compared to UAV at the orchard scale and the highest PBias of 79 % and 61 % were observed for Sentinel-2 during November at a uniform and highly varying orchard respectively. All the satellite platforms capture spatiotemporal canopy vigor have lower PBias for the uniform orchard compared to the highly varying orchard. At the pixel level, the satellite platforms overestimate low vigor and underestimate high vigor, especially during the late season. The satellite platforms have a significant correlation with the UAV (p < 0.05) at both the orchard sites. Additionally, the Pleiades and Planet platforms are capable of delineating similar MUZs with the UAV during the mid-season, which where applicable for foliar chemical applications. However, Sentinel-2 was found to be unsatisfactory.

Modeling in-orchard (apple) weather using open field weather data

This project is discussed in details on Chapter 3 of Karisma Yumnam's MS Thesis, Washington State University 2023

Authors/Contributors: Karisma Yumnam, Matthew D. Can, Lav R. Khot

The in-orchard weather often differs from the open-field weather station data commonly used in developing the grower decision support tools. Orchard site, training system, and in season management along with tree physiology can cause microclimate variations and have orchard effects. Therefore, the study developed in-orchard air temperature (AT), relative humidity (RH), and wind speed (WS) prediction models considering open field weather data, modern apple orchard site (six), training system (Solaxe, Bi-axis, V-trellis), and in season management (evaporative cooling). Multiple Linear Regression (MLR), and two machine learning (ML) models namely K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) and Random Forest (RF) models were trained and tested using multi-site weather data. Overall, the RF model outperformed other two models with root mean square error (RMSE) of 0.51 ℃, 3.14 %, and 0.21 m s-1 for AT, RH, and WS predictions, respectively. The respective RF models reduced the overall open-field and in-orchard offsets error by 62 %, 63 %, and 85 %. Keywords: Apple production, Modern orchard systems, Microclimates, Orchard effect, Hyperlocal weather predictions, Machine learning

Evaluation of precipitation products as inputs for a multi-site calibration of SWAT with satellite-based evapotranspiration in a data-sparse, low lying catchment in Benin

This project is discussed in details on Chapter 4 of Karisma Yumnam's M.Tech thesis, Indian Institute of Technology, Roorkee 2021

This work was conducted in collaboration with Technical University Munich and funded by the DAAD

This study examined an alternative method to calibrate the SWAT eco-hydrological model using available satellite-based GLEAM evapotranspiration data for the data-sparse Oueme river basin in Benin, West Africa. The Hargreaves, Priestley–Taylor and Penman-Monteith equations in SWAT cause the different AET values obtained. Twelve different calibrations were implemented with different ground-based, satellite and reanalysis precipitation products to obtain a high-performing model for the Cotonou City, Benin. All the calibration results may indicate that the coefficient of determination may not be a good statistical parameter for evaluating the SWAT model calibrated for evapotranspiration. In general, SWAT calibration using the Penman-Monteith method have lower Pbias than the Hargreaves Method, indicating the robustness of the Penman-Monteith method over the Hargreaves method. Interestingly, the SWAT model calibrated using TRMM inputs outperforms gauge based simulations at few subbasins. In general, the SWAT model estimates low evapotranspiration during the dry season. Comparatively, the Penman-Monteith Method of evapotranspiration estimation in SWAT outperform the Hargreaves evapotranspiration estimation method during December and January.
